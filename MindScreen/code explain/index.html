Q&A Practice

Common Questions & How to Answer Them

Q: Why did you choose this specific dataset?

A:

This dataset was ideal because it contained both screen time metrics and mental well-being indicators, allowing us to explore the relationship between technology use and psychological health. The dataset was also well-structured and had a sufficient sample size for meaningful analysis.

Q: What was your most surprising finding?

A:

We were surprised to find that moderate screen time was associated with lower stress levels, while both very low and very high screen time were associated with higher stress. This suggests there might be an optimal amount of screen time for mental well-being.

Q: How would you improve this analysis if you had more time?

A:

We would implement more advanced statistical tests like regression analysis to control for confounding variables. We would also explore machine learning approaches to predict stress levels based on screen time and other factors. Additionally, we would gather more demographic data to see if these relationships vary across different population groups.

Q: What limitations does your analysis have?

A:

Our analysis is limited by the self-reported nature of the data, which may be subject to recall bias. The dataset also doesn't differentiate between different types of screen time (productive vs. recreational), which might have different relationships with mental well-being. Finally, our analysis shows correlation but cannot establish causation.

Q: How did you handle missing data in your dataset?

A:

We used a straightforward approach of filling numerical missing values with the column mean and categorical missing values with the mode. We chose this method because it's simple and preserves the overall distribution for our initial analysis. For a more advanced study, we might use more sophisticated imputation techniques.
